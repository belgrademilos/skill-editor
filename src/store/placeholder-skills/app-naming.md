# App Naming Skill

Your job is to guide the user through a structured naming process and deliver 100 distinctive name candidates.

## How This Works

The process has two phases:
1. **Discovery** — Walk the user through the Diamond exercise to deeply understand what they're building and what the name needs to accomplish
2. **Generation** — Produce 100 name candidates organized by creative direction, with rationale

Do NOT skip the discovery phase or rush to generating names. The Diamond exercise is what separates good names from generic ones. The quality of names you generate depends entirely on how well you understand the product and what "winning" looks like.

---

## Phase 1: The Diamond ◆ Exercise

Walk the user through these four questions, one at a time. After each answer, reflect back what you heard and probe deeper before moving on. You're looking for experiential and behavioral cues — not corporate mission statements.

### ◆ Top: "How do you define winning for this product?"
What does success look like? Market dominance? A specific user behavior? A feeling people associate with the product? Get concrete — "people recommend it to friends" is better than "market leadership."

### ◆ Right: "What do you already have going for you?"
Strengths, assets, differentiation, unfair advantages. What's already true about this product that a name could lean into?

### ◆ Bottom: "What do you still need to win?"
Gaps, missing capabilities, things the name might need to compensate for or signal. If the product is early-stage, the name might need to carry more weight in establishing credibility or category.

### ◆ Left: "What does the name need to say — or make people feel?"
This is the most important question. Push beyond vague adjectives. Ask: "How do you want the market to *behave* toward you? What vibes should this name trigger?" Get at the experience and emotion, not just attributes.

### After the Diamond

Once you have all four answers, synthesize what you've learned into a brief **Naming Brief** — a few sentences capturing:
- The core experience/behavior the name should evoke
- The competitive landscape (what names already exist in this space)
- Any constraints (technical, cultural, linguistic)
- The "energy" of the name — is it playful? Authoritative? Warm? Sharp?

Share this brief with the user and confirm it captures the essence before moving to generation.

---

## Phase 2: Name Generation

Now generate **100 name candidates**. This is about volume and range — suspend your own judgment and explore widely. Unexpected and polarizing names often outperform "safe" ones in competitive markets.

### Organize names into creative directions

Group the 100 names into 8-12 thematic directions. Each direction is a different creative angle on the naming brief. For example:

- **Compounds** — Two familiar words combined to multiply meaning (like PowerBook, Windsurf, Snapchat)
- **Sound-driven** — Names chosen for how they feel in the mouth and ear, using sound symbolism:
  - "V" sounds = vibrant, velocity
  - "B" sounds = reliable, grounded
  - "Z" sounds = electric, edgy
  - "X" sounds = innovative, cutting-edge
  - Hard consonants (K, T, P) = crisp, decisive
  - Soft sounds (L, M, S) = smooth, approachable
- **Metaphor** — Names borrowed from unrelated domains that capture the right feeling (like Amazon, Apple, Slack)
- **Invented words** — Completely new words that sound right for the brand (like Spotify, Hulu, Zapier)
- **Action/verb-based** — Names that imply what the product does or how it makes you feel (like Sprint, Dash, Kindle)
- **Twist on familiar** — Real words slightly altered or used in a new context (like Figma, Notion, Linear)

Not every direction will apply to every product. Choose directions that make sense for the naming brief. Each direction should have 8-15 names.

### For each direction, include:
- A one-line description of the creative angle
- The list of names
- A brief note (1 sentence) on the standout 2-3 names explaining why they work

### Generation principles

- **Distinctive over descriptive.** "Azure" beats "Cloud Pro." Descriptive names blend in; distinctive names compound advantage over time.
- **Don't play it safe.** The greatest risk in naming is picking something forgettable. Polarizing and unexpected wins in competitive markets.
- **Import from unrelated fields.** Some of the best names come from completely different domains — mythology, geography, science, music, cooking. Cross-pollinate freely.
- **Think about how it lives in context.** Would this look good on an app icon? In a sentence? As a verb ("just Slack me")? Names that work as verbs or fit naturally into speech have a huge edge.
- **Sound matters as much as meaning.** Say each name out loud. Does it feel right? Is it easy to say? Does the sound match the energy from the naming brief?
- **Don't worry about domains.** In the AI era, the exact .com match is far less important than the right name. Generate fearlessly — domain issues can be solved later.

## After Presenting the 100

Once you've shared all 100 names, help the user narrow down:

1. Ask them to pick 5-10 that jump out — gut reaction, no overthinking
2. For their picks, offer a quick competitive gut-check: *"If your competitor launched with this name tomorrow, what would you think?"*
3. Suggest they mock up their top 3-5 visually — in an app store listing, on a landing page, in a conversation ("Have you tried [Name]?") — to see how the name lives in the real world

Remind them: the name that feels slightly uncomfortable or bold is often the right one. Safe names are the riskiest choice.
