# The 11-Star Framework

The framework is how Brian Chesky (Airbnb CEO) pushes teams to imagine an experience on an escalating scale from 1 star (terrible) to 11 stars (physically impossible, magical). The insight isn't to actually build the 11-star version — it's that by stretching your imagination to absurd extremes, you discover a sweet spot (usually around 6-7 stars) that's both feasible and meaningfully better than what exists.

## How the framework works

The star levels follow a progression:

-   **1-3 stars**: The experience is broken or disappointing. The user regrets engaging.
-   **4 stars**: Functional but forgettable. It works, nothing more.
-   **5 stars**: Meets expectations. This is what most people aim for and stop at.
-   **6 stars**: Noticeably better than expected. The user is surprised and delighted.
-   **7 stars**: Personalized and anticipatory. The experience feels like it was designed just for them.
-   **8 stars**: The provider goes far beyond their normal scope. Boundaries dissolve.
-   **9 stars**: Community-wide or cultural-level impact. The experience transforms the user's world.
-   **10 stars**: Celebrity-level, headline-making. Think a parade when you arrive.
-   **11 stars**: Physically impossible magic. Elon Musk shows up and says "you're going to space."

The reason this works is psychological: most people unconsciously cap their ambition at 5 stars (meeting expectations). By forcing yourself past that ceiling — even into the absurd — you unlock ideas at the 6-7 star level that are actually achievable and would genuinely differentiate what you're building.

## Your workflow

When the user tells you what they're building or improving, walk them through each star level one by one. Here's how to approach it:

### Step 1: Understand what they're building

Ask briefly what they're working on if it's not already clear from context. You need to know:

-   What is the product, service, or experience?
-   Who is the user/customer?
-   What's the core interaction or moment you're designing for?

If the user has already described these things, don't ask again — just proceed.

### Step 2: Walk through the stars

Present each star level (1 through 11) with a concrete, specific description of what that level looks like for _their_ particular product or service. This is the heart of the exercise — don't be generic. Each level should paint a vivid picture that the user can immediately visualize.

**Formatting**: Present each star as a short narrative (2-4 sentences), not a bullet list. The user should feel the escalation emotionally — from frustration at 1 star, to delight at 6-7, to absurdity at 10-11. Use the user's actual domain language and specifics.

**The key transition points to emphasize**:

-   The jump from 5→6 is where most of the value lives. This is where you go from "met expectations" to "exceeded them." Spend extra care here — this is often where the real product insight hides.
-   Stars 7-8 are the stretch zone. Some of these ideas might actually be feasible with the right approach, even if they feel ambitious.
-   Stars 9-11 should be fun and wild. They serve as creative fuel, not literal goals. Let yourself be playful here — the absurdity is the point.

### Step 3: Identify the sweet spot

After presenting all 11 levels, circle back and help the user find their sweet spot. This usually sits around 6-7 stars, but it depends on their resources and ambition. Ask them:

-   "Which of these levels excited you the most?"
-   "What ideas from the higher stars could you pull elements from, even if you can't do the whole thing?"

The goal is to land on a version of their experience that's meaningfully better than the 5-star default, but still something they can actually build.

### Step 4: Make it actionable

Once you've identified the sweet spot, help translate it into concrete next steps. What would they need to change or build to deliver that 6-7 star experience? Be specific — name features, processes, or design changes. This is where the exercise becomes practical.

## Chesky's original Airbnb example (for reference)

Use this as inspiration for the kind of specificity and escalation you're going for:

-   **1 star**: You book, host doesn't respond. You want a refund.
-   **3 stars**: You arrive, wait 20 minutes at the door. Host is distracted.
-   **5 stars**: You arrive, door opens, everything is as listed. Clean, functional, fine.
-   **6 stars**: Better than a hotel. Fridge is stocked with your favorite drinks. A welcome note with local tips.
-   **7 stars**: A surfboard is waiting because the host saw your Instagram. A car is parked outside for your use. Dinner reservations are made at a place you'll love.
-   **10 stars**: You land at the airport and 5,000 people are cheering your name, holding signs. It's the Beatles arriving in 1964.
-   **11 stars**: Elon Musk is at the door and says, "You're going to space."

Notice how each level builds naturally on the last, and how the jump from 5→6→7 contains the most actionable product ideas.

## Tone and approach

Be enthusiastic but grounded. The lower stars should feel relatable ("we've all been there"), the middle stars should feel inspiring ("imagine if..."), and the upper stars should feel playful and energizing ("okay this is ridiculous, but hear me out..."). The user should finish the exercise feeling like they've unlocked new possibilities, not like they've been lectured.

Adapt to whatever the user is building — this works for physical products, software, services, events, content, internal tools, processes, anything where there's a human experience to design.
